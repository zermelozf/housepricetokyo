A Data-Driven Approach to Tokyo Real Estate

Key Factors Affecting Property Values

Location, Location, Location

As in any major city, location is the most significant factor in determining property values. Our analysis of MLIT data shows that properties in central Tokyo can be worth up to 150% more than similar properties in outer areas.

Location Factors

The location of a property is one of the most significant factors affecting its value in Tokyo. Our analysis reveals several key location-based factors that influence property prices:

  • Walking Time to Station: One of the most important factors in Tokyo's real estate market is the walking time to the nearest train station. Our analysis shows a complex relationship between walking time and property values, with 10 minutes as the reference point. The visualization reveals several interesting patterns:

  • Optimal Range: Properties within 0-5 minutes of a station command the highest premiums, with values up to +¥124,758 per square meter above the reference point. This reflects the high demand for extremely convenient locations.
  • Steep Decline: Beyond 15 minutes, there's a significant negative impact on property values, with properties 20+ minutes away showing decreases of over ¥500,000 per square meter compared to the reference point. The trend line shows a clear negative correlation between walking time and property value, with an average devaluation rate of approximately ¥15,000 per square meter per minute of additional walking time.
  • Variability: There's notable variation in the impact at similar walking times (shown by the spread of points around the trend line), suggesting that other factors like station importance, train lines, and neighborhood characteristics also play a role. The trend line helps visualize the general pattern despite this variability.

This data demonstrates the premium that buyers are willing to pay for convenience and accessibility to public transportation in Tokyo. Properties within a 5-minute walk of a station command the highest values, while those beyond 15 minutes show significant discounts. The trend line shows that, on average, each additional minute of walking time has a substantial impact on property values, though the relationship is not perfectly linear due to the influence of other factors.

Building Type and Depreciation

One of the most interesting findings from our analysis is how different building materials depreciate over time. Using MLIT data, we were able to model the depreciation curves for different construction types, with NA (unknown/other) as the reference point. The graph below shows how the value per square meter changes over time for each type of construction:

The analysis reveals several key insights about building depreciation in Tokyo:

  • Initial Values: Steel structures (鉄骨造) command the highest initial value at +¥93,437 per square meter above the reference, followed by light steel structures (軽量鉄骨造) at +¥56,795 per square meter. Interestingly, wooden structures (木造) start with a negative value of -¥9,408 per square meter compared to the reference.
  • Depreciation Rates: Light steel structures show the fastest depreciation rate at -¥12,059 per square meter per year, followed by steel structures at -¥8,526 per year, and wooden structures at -¥8,197 per year. This means that while steel structures start with a much higher value, they lose value more quickly than wooden structures.
  • Value Crossover: Due to the combination of higher initial values and faster depreciation rates, steel and light steel structures eventually cross over with wooden structures in terms of value. This is an important consideration when evaluating older properties.

These findings suggest that while steel structures might be more attractive for new construction, wooden structures might be a better choice for long-term investment due to their lower initial cost and slower depreciation rate. Light steel structures, despite their lower initial cost compared to full steel structures, show the fastest depreciation rate, which might make them less attractive for long-term investments.

Land Characteristics

The physical characteristics of the land itself play a crucial role in determining its value. Our analysis revealed several key factors:

  • Land Size (土地面積): While larger plots generally command higher total prices, the price per square meter often decreases as the plot size increases. This is known as the "plottage effect" - the ability to build larger, more efficient structures on bigger plots.
  • Floor Ratio (容積率): The floor ratio, which determines how much building area can be constructed relative to the land area, has a significant impact on land value. Our analysis shows that the standard reference ratio of 150% serves as a baseline, with higher ratios generally increasing land value and lower ratios decreasing it. The chart below illustrates this relationship:

As shown in the chart, the floor ratio has a non-linear relationship with land value. While ratios below 100% tend to decrease land value, ratios above 200% show increasingly positive impacts on value. This reflects the development potential of the land and its ability to generate more usable space.

  • Building Ratio (建ぺい率): The building ratio, which determines how much of the land area can be covered by buildings, also significantly affects land value. Our analysis shows that the standard reference ratio of 60% serves as a baseline, with higher ratios generally increasing land value and lower ratios decreasing it. The chart below illustrates this relationship:

As shown in the chart, the building ratio has a clear impact on land value. Ratios below 60% tend to decrease land value, while higher ratios can significantly increase it. This reflects the development potential of the land and its ability to accommodate larger buildings.

  • Land Shape and Topography: The shape of the land has a significant impact on its value. Our analysis shows that using square-shaped land (正方形) as the reference point, landlocked properties (袋地等) and irregular shapes (不整形) have the most negative impact on value, with decreases of over ¥62,000 per square meter. On the other hand, regular rectangular shapes (長方形) and near-rectangular shapes (ほぼ長方形) have a positive impact of around ¥2,500-2,900 per square meter. This reflects the importance of buildability and efficient land use in determining property values.

  • Land Orientation: The orientation of the land relative to the road also affects its value. Our analysis shows that using east-facing properties (東) as the reference point, properties with no road frontage (接面道路無) have the most negative impact on value, with a decrease of over ¥238,000 per square meter. This reflects the strong preference for south-facing properties in Japan due to better sunlight exposure.
