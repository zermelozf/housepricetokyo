Decoding Tokyo Property Prices: What Really Matters?

Using Machine Learning to Uncover Key Value Drivers

By Arnaud R. • March 2024

The Background Story

It all started with a simple question: say you are looking at a 5000万円 property listing (on suumo.jp for example), how much of that is the value of the house and how much is the land?

I had been looking at listings for a while already, and was getting serious about making an offer. But at the back of my mind, I was feeling frustrated that I didn't really understand what makes a property valuable in Tokyo. I mean, I'm no a real estate investor, nor are my friends and most of the people who take the leap and buy a family house in one of the 23 wards. So in that specific insntace, it's probably OK to not know, I suppose. But still, where there is data, there is a way!

So with that in mind, I started looking for a dataset covering the Tokyo rReal Estate Market and ended up uncovering the MLIT 不動産情報ライブラリ dataset: a treasure trove!

In the following article, I'll use statistical analysis to try and understand what drives property value in Tokyo. I'll start by briefly describing the techniques and assumptions I made, and then spend the rest of the article presenting the results through various charts and graphs.

Making some assumptions

Given the variety of features available and my goal of understanding what drives value, I chose to stick with Linear Regression (for robustness and interpretability) and spend some time on modeling/feature engineering. So yes, this article is 100% LLM free!

In terms of modeling, I also kept it simple and made the following assumptions:

  • The price of a listing in the MLIT dataset is split between land and house value
  • The price of the house is proportional to the floor area and the building type and year of construction might increase or decrease the price per square meter
  • Similarily, the land value is proportional to the plot size, and the price per square meter might increase or decrease depending on the land characteristics such as shape, orientation, distance to the station, etc.

When to buy?

One factor not shown in the chart above is the time of purchase. The year the property is bought also has an impact on the price and, according to the model, the earlier the better. In fact, the model assesses that the annualized rate of price growth is about 3.5% per year on average.

The Lasso regression analysis also uncovered interesting patterns in how different building materials depreciate over time. The model helped us quantify these relationships:

Steel and Light Steel are unsurprisingly the most durable and thus cost the most. Interestingly however, they depreciate faster than wooden structures and while they retain an advantage overall for durability, the Wooden structures are faring surprisingly well in my opinion. The Price of Convenience

One interesting result from of the Lasso model is the precise quantification of the impact of walking time to the nearest train station.

In a variant of the model shown above, I allowed the $\beta$ coefficients associated to walk time to be dummy coded minute by minute. This would theoretically allow for a non-linear relationship to be picked up. However, as you can see in the chart, the relationship seems pretty stable and linear with each minute closer to the station will add about ¥10,000 per square meter to the price of the property. This means a 100 square meter property will be worth about ¥10 million more if it's located right next to the station.

The Construction Ratios

Floor Ratio (容積率)

Let's be quick with this one. The floor ratio is the ratio of the total floor area of a building to the total land area of the property. It is a measure of how much of the land is used for building purposes and is a legal requirement when building. The higher the floor ratio, the bigger the house you can build on the property. Unsuprisingly, higher floor ratios are associated with higher property values.

Building Ratio (建ぺい率)

This ratio is similar to the floor ratio but it is a measure of how much of the land is covered by the building and is also a legal requirement when building. The higher the building ratio, the more of the land is covered by the building and so the more expensive it is.

Land Shape and Orientation

Land Shape

The learnt coefficients seem consistent with the intuition that square-shaped land is more valuable than irregularly shaped land. Also, the model is flagging landlocked properties (袋地等) as much less valuable. This is an important factor to take into account and sometimes the reason that explains why a listing on suumo.jp is so cheap.

Land Orientation

Land orientation does not seem to have a big impact on the price of a property. However, you should definitely avoid properties with no access (接面道路無). These types of properies are often not buildable anymore due to a change is legislation and so the value of the land is dramatically decreased (for good reason)..
