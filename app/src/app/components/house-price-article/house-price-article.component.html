Modeling Tokyo Real Estate

The MLIT dataset is a treasure trove of information about the Tokyo real estate market. It contains over 100,000 property records, with features ranging from location to building specifications.

Given the variety of features available and my goal of understanding what drives value, I chose to stick with Linear Regression (for robustness and interpretability) and spend some time on modeling/feature engineering. So yes, this article is 100% LLM free!

In terms of modeling, I also kept it simple and made the following assumptions: - The price of a listing in the MLIT dataset is split between land and house value - The price of the house is proportional to the floor area and the building type and year of construction might increase or decrease the price per square meter - Similarily, the land value is proportional to the plot size, and the price per square meter might increase or decrease depending on the land characteristics such as shape, orientation, distance to the station, etc.

As shown in the chart, both land and house values in Tokyo have shown significant upward trends, but with some interesting differences:

  • Land Values: From 2013 to 2024, the average land value per square meter has increased from ¥425,101 to ¥619,906, representing a total increase of approximately 46%. This trend highlights the strong demand for land in Tokyo and the potential for long-term value appreciation.
  • House Values: House values have shown an even more dramatic increase, rising from ¥60,523 to ¥180,541 per square meter, representing a total increase of approximately 198%. This suggests that the value of buildings themselves has increased significantly, possibly due to improvements in construction quality, energy efficiency, and modern amenities.

The chart also reveals some interesting patterns, such as the temporary dip in land values in 2014-2015, and the accelerated growth in both land and house values in recent years. These trends are important to consider when evaluating the potential return on investment in Tokyo's real estate market.

Our Data-Driven Approach

To understand what truly drives property values in Tokyo, we analyzed extensive data from the MLIT using Lasso regression. This machine learning technique is particularly well-suited for our analysis because it:

  • Identifies Key Factors: Lasso automatically selects the most important variables from hundreds of potential factors, helping us focus on what really matters.
  • Handles Complex Relationships: It can capture both linear and non-linear relationships between variables, crucial for understanding the nuanced Tokyo real estate market.
  • Provides Quantifiable Insights: The model gives us precise coefficients that tell us exactly how much each factor affects property values.

Our analysis included over 100,000 property records from across Tokyo, with features ranging from location characteristics to building specifications. The Lasso model helped us identify the most significant factors, which we'll explore in detail below.

Key Factors Affecting Property Values

Our Lasso regression analysis identified several key factors that significantly influence property values in Tokyo. Let's explore each of these factors in detail, with insights directly from our machine learning model.

Location, Location, Location

The Lasso model confirmed what many already suspect: location is paramount in Tokyo's real estate market. However, it also revealed some surprising nuances about how different location factors interact with property values.

Walking Time to Station

One of the most significant findings from our Lasso analysis was the precise impact of walking time to the nearest train station. The model identified this as one of the strongest predictors of property value, with a clear non-linear relationship:

The Lasso coefficients revealed several key insights about walking time's impact:

  • Premium for Convenience: Properties within 5 minutes of a station command the highest premium, with Lasso coefficients showing values up to +¥124,758 per square meter above the reference point.
  • Steep Value Decline: The model identified a significant negative impact beyond 15 minutes, with properties 20+ minutes away showing decreases of over ¥500,000 per square meter. The Lasso coefficients helped us quantify this relationship, showing an average devaluation rate of approximately ¥15,000 per square meter per minute of additional walking time.
  • Complex Interactions: The Lasso model revealed that walking time interacts with other factors, such as station importance and train lines, creating variations in the impact at similar walking times.

Building Type and Depreciation

Our Lasso regression analysis uncovered fascinating patterns in how different building materials depreciate over time. The model helped us quantify these relationships with remarkable precision:

The Lasso coefficients revealed several key insights about building depreciation:

  • Initial Value Premiums: The model identified significant initial value differences:
    • Steel structures (鉄骨造): +¥93,437 per square meter
    • Light steel structures (軽量鉄骨造): +¥56,795 per square meter
    • Wooden structures (木造): -¥9,408 per square meter
  • Depreciation Rates: Lasso helped us quantify the annual depreciation rates:
    • Light steel structures: -¥12,059 per square meter per year
    • Steel structures: -¥8,526 per square meter per year
    • Wooden structures: -¥8,197 per square meter per year
  • Value Crossover Points: The model's coefficients allowed us to calculate precise crossover points where different building types become more or less valuable relative to each other.
